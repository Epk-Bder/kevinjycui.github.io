James K 16-Apr-19 09:04 PM
Edgar uses an apostrophe as he’s calling out to nature to develop the theme of nature and society, and how they differ from each other. Edgar is questioning why he is not being seen as a “legitimate” son of King Lear, he believes that he is just as gifted in mind and body as legitimate children. He argues that he is born in the same way anyone else is, but society has branded bastards sinful. He dwells on the word “bastard” and justifies it to ensure that he is the same, if not better than Edgar. Edgar uses an alliteration to emphasize his obsession with the word and its meaning. This whole soliloquy develops the theme of illegitimacy and supremacy in society in the sense that society believes bastards to be inferior, however, Edmund believes quite the opposite.
The fool is comparing King Lear’s status as a king and ruler of his kingdom to a boiled egg, more so the yolk. He states that when you cut your kingdom into 2 parts and give them away as he did, he was as foolish and stupid as someone who carries their donkey instead of letting the donkey carry him as he now has no power or control over anything. This quote deeply ties in with the theme of blindness throughout the book with King Lear not seeing the bigger picture, additionally, this is incredibly ironic as The Fool must explain to King Lear the stupidity of what he has done, when The Fool himself is supposed to act in such a way.
The Fool is explaining to Kent how continuing to serve the now mad king is futile and will get him nowhere, he goes into further detail by using the analogy of a wheel rolling down a hill, when you try to hold on to it, you may end up breaking your neck, however, if you see a wheel going uphill, grab onto it as a wise man will give better advice than a fool, and that he only wants the stupid to follow his advice. This is very ironic, due to the fact that The Fool seems to be giving advice to those who are lost, and someone like himself is only giving advice to those who share his name, fools. This ties into the the theme of blindness as well, because The Fool is guiding the blind and the foolish.
